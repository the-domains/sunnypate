Real self - ideal self - fully functional self

"Most of the luxuries and many of the so-called comforts of life are not only not indispensable, but positive hindrances to the elevation of mankind."— Thoreau

Carl Rogers who I discussed in "Defining Self-Actualization" discussed peoples growth potential, where ones aim was to harmonize the "real self" with the "ideal self" to emerge into a "fully functioning person."

Most of the time environmental factors affect people's ability to become self-actualized because there focus is merely just to survive. Factors such as poverty, drug abuse, broken families, mental or physical illness and more can all impact what is at the forefront of our minds and create a road block towards us reaching our full potential. Environmental factors though should not become an excuse for why you can't reach your full potential. Many people have overcome adversity to achieve great things, some examples are: Abraham Lincoln, Oprah Winfrey, Franklin Roosevelt, Malcolm X, and Tennessee Williams - just to name a few.

Defining "Real-Self"

Psychologists define the real self as who we actually are, how we think, feel, look, and act.

Defining "Ideal-Self"

The ideal self is how we want to be, it's an idealized image we developed based on what we have learned and experienced. This image can include components of what our parents have taught us, what society promotes, what we admire in other people, and what we think is in our best interest.

The closer the real self and ideal self are aligned the more at peace one will be.

A fully functioning person is in touch with their deepest feelings and desires, according to Carl Rogers. They understand their emotions, trust their gut, and are unconditionally positive. Rogers believed that a fully functioning person was a person who was always working toward becoming self-actualized. A fully functioning person is open to life experiences and adventures, can express themselves freely, is comfortable in their skin and does not base their self worth on external factors.

"He is able to live in his feelings and reactions of the moment. He is not bound by the structure of his past learning's, but these are a present resource for him insofar as they relate to the experience of the moment. He lives freely, subjectively, in an existential confrontation of this moment in life." (Rogers, 1962)

When you are a fully functioning person you have a flexible view on self and experiences. Ones sense of self is consistent with emotions and experiences, fully functioning individuals move in the direction towards greater growth and fulfillment of one's own potential.

Some Characteristics of Fully Functioning people:

-Ability to interpret experiences accurately

-Lack of defensiveness

-Open to new experiences

-Open to feedback

-Lives in harmony with others

-Unconditional self-regard

Some ways to start living as a fully functioning individual:

-Be present: live in the now. Eckhart Tolle says: “Unease, anxiety, tension, stress, worry — all forms of fear — are cause by too much future, and not enough presence. Guilt, regret, resentment, grievances, sadness, bitterness, and all forms of non-forgiveness are caused by too much past, and not enough presence.”

-Trust your Intuition: Learn to love and trust yourself, be tolerant and compassionate in every situation you encounter. If you start taking time for yourself and start listening to your inner needs you will be more in tune with what your body is trying to tell you and start trusting it.

-Be open: You are responsible for your life and the way it turns out, but don't confuse this statement with control - we are not in control but we can guide our lives towards living our best lives possible. When you are living your passion success will be a byproduct in your personal and professional life.
